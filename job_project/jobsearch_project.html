Job Search Analysis — Where Time Goes & Where to Invest It

Data-driven breakdown of 321 applications, 44 interviews, and 19 second rounds — Munich, Jun 2025 – Mar 2026

Project Overview

After tracking every job application in a structured spreadsheet over 9 months, I ran a full data analysis to understand where effort was well-spent and where it was systematically wasted. The dataset covers 321 applications across role categories, seniority levels, and company sectors — with conversion rates calculated for each dimension.

The methodology mirrors a standard marketing funnel analysis: impressions → click-through → qualified lead → conversion. Applied to job search, each stage reveals where the pipeline breaks — and what to fix.

Business Applications of This Framework

The same analytical approach used here applies directly to any performance funnel in marketing or sales:

  • Campaign attribution: which channels generate qualified leads vs vanity impressions
  • CRM pipeline analysis: where deals stall — prospecting, demo, proposal, or close
  • E-commerce conversion: identifying drop-off stages across the purchase funnel
  • Resource allocation: reallocating budget from low-CR channels to high-CR ones

The core question is always the same: which inputs produce the best outputs per unit of effort?

Conversion Rate by Seniority Level

Interview CR by level — ordered by performance

Trainee (35%, n=20): highest CR but very few positions exist in the German market for non-students. Not scalable.
Junior (7.3%, n=82): worst category by CR. In Germany, "Junior" typically implies 1–2 years of direct experience. Competing here as a Berufseinsteiger was a structural disadvantage — not a skills issue.
Regular (14.4%, n=187): most efficient category by volume. These roles don't require prior experience in that exact title, which gives career-changers and Berufseinsteiger a fairer shot.

Conversion Rate by Role Category

CR per category — all 12 categories, real counts from CSV

>20% CR — strong 10–20% — average borderline <10% — weak
Performance / SEO (6.1%, n=33): 33 applications is a statistically reliable sample. The CR is definitively weak — these roles demand specialist portfolios (Google Ads certified with campaign management history, SEO tools at expert level). Stop applying here.
Content / Social (6.2%, n=16): same story. These roles want content creators with portfolio-heavy social accounts, not analytics profiles.
Analytics / Tracking (22.2%, n=9): small sample but highest efficiency per application. The GA4/GTM/Python stack is the differentiator — and competition is thin compared to 200+ applicants on generic roles.
CRM / Email (7.1%, n=14): surprisingly weak despite Salesforce experience. Likely explanation: these roles seek marketing automation tools (Klaviyo, Brevo, Mailchimp) rather than CRM Sales Cloud — a different category of tool.

Key Findings

Three patterns emerge clearly from the data:

Finding 1 — The funnel breaks at the final step, not the CV. A 13.7% application-to-interview rate is above market average for a Berufseinsteiger in Munich. The issue is converting final rounds into offers — a problem of domain-specific experience depth, not profile quality.
Finding 2 — Volume without targeting is inefficient. Performance/SEO and Content/Social absorbed 49 combined applications and produced only 3 interviews. That effort, redirected to Analytics and CS roles, would statistically have yielded 10–12 interviews instead.
Finding 3 — The dataset contains a structural bias. ~270 of 321 applications were submitted while still in Ausbildung. The completion of the IHK qualification (Feb 2026) removes the primary hiring blocker. The CR figures for the most recent 50 applications will diverge significantly once enough time has passed for outcomes to materialise.

This mirrors a principle applicable in any performance channel: measuring vanity metrics (applications sent) instead of quality-adjusted conversion rates leads to systematically wrong resource allocation decisions. The fix is not sending more applications — it is sending the right ones to the right segments.

Dataset Details

  • Rows: 321 applications
  • Period: June 2025 – March 2026
  • Location: Munich, Germany (primarily) + remote roles
  • Key fields: Firma, Stelle, Status, Beschreibung
  • Status values: Beworben, Absage, Keine Antwort, Gespräch (<30 / >30 min), Absage nach Gespräch
  • Categorisation: role categories and sectors derived via keyword classification in Python
  • Limitation: 207/321 companies could not be reliably sector-classified from name alone — sector CR is directional only
